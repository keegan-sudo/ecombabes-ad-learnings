# Ad Creative Learnings

## What We Did
Analyzed 7 Facebook video ads for EcomBabes across two batches using the `/watch` skill (frame extraction + Whisper transcription). Scored each ad 1–100 for likelihood of being a winning creative. After each batch, compared predictions against actual performance data provided by the client.

---

## Ads Analyzed & Results

| Ad | Score | Actual Result |
|---|---|---|
| 375 — "$1,000 in 30 Days" (chalkboard) | 64 | ✅ Winner |
| 810 — Editorial curly-hair hook | 44 | ❌ Loser |
| AD2 HOOK 2 — Gender pay gap stats | 78 | ❌ Loser |
| 5-9 Ad — "Five to Nine Strategy" | 68 | ✅ Winner |
| 363 — "Three Years Ago at Target" | 58 | ✅ Winner |
| 345 — "This Bag Is Worth $100K" | 74 | ❌ Loser |
| AD3 HOOK 2 — "Simplest Way to Make Money" | 49 | ❌ Loser |

**Prediction accuracy: 4/7 correct (57%)**

---

## What the Winners Had in Common

1. **Specific number + specific timeframe in the hook**
   - "$1,000 in 30 days" — instantly answers "what's in it for me" before the viewer can scroll
   - This is the highest-leverage element in the first 3 seconds

2. **Deep audience empathy — hooks in their lived reality**
   - "Five to Nine Strategy" reframes the 9-to-5 grind they already feel every day — clever wordplay on something they know
   - "Three years ago I was working at this Target in Nebraska" — filmed from the actual parking lot — puts the viewer directly inside a story they recognize as real
   - The target audience is women stuck in jobs, supporting families. Winners meet them exactly where they are

3. **Authentic, unpolished, grounded storytelling**
   - Alyssa filming from her car outside Target is more powerful than a studio setup
   - "Stay-at-home cat mom" is a specific, funny, relatable line that sticks

4. **No setup required** — you understand the promise within 5 seconds

---

## What the Losers Had in Common

1. **Clever curiosity gaps that don't deliver fast enough**
   - "This bag is worth $100,000" — scored 74/100, it lost. The misdirect (Hermes → plain bag) is creative but cold traffic doesn't wait for the payoff
   - Creativity ≠ conversions on Facebook

2. **Intellectual/data-driven hooks**
   - Gender pay gap stats (83 cents on the dollar) — scored 78/100, it lost. Requires the viewer to process information before feeling anything. Cold traffic needs emotion first, data second

3. **Generic claims with no specificity**
   - "Simplest way to make money from the internet" — no number, no timeframe, no differentiation

4. **Visual/aesthetic hooks**
   - Editorial crumpled-paper style — looks interesting but doesn't communicate value

---

## Where My Scoring Framework Was Wrong

- **Overweighted:** production quality, visual creativity, clever misdirects, emotional sophistication
- **Underweighted:** specificity of the hook claim (number + timeframe), how directly the hook mirrors the audience's daily pain, authenticity of the storytelling setup
- **Key insight:** For cold Facebook traffic targeting women seeking financial independence, proven direct-response formula beats creative every time. The audience doesn't reward cleverness — they respond to feeling seen

---

## Updated Scoring Framework

When evaluating a hook for this audience, weight in this order:

| Factor | Weight | What to Look For |
|---|---|---|
| **Specificity** | 40% | Dollar amount + timeframe named in first 5 seconds |
| **Audience mirror** | 35% | Reflects exact daily reality of a woman stuck in a job, supporting a family, feeling financially behind |
| **Proof credibility** | 15% | Specific, visual, hard-to-fake social proof (Shopify dashboards, real names, actual locations) |
| **Production/length** | 10% | Almost irrelevant if the above three are strong |

# Paid Ad Creatives — General Learnings (The Plug Playbook)

Source: The Plug's Winning Creatives Playbook (2026)

---

## 1. The Hook Is Everything

You have **1.7 seconds** to stop the scroll. The hook is the single most important element in the entire creative — not the offer, not the CTA.

**Hook formulas that work:**

| Hook Type | Formula | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Problem Hook | "Why do [X%] of people who [do Y] never [get result]?" | "Why do 80% of people who buy phone accessories never use them after week one?" |
| Contradiction Hook | "I [stopped/quit doing X] and [positive result] has never been better." | "I stopped buying expensive chargers and my battery health has never been better." |
| Direct Call-Out | "If you're still [doing X], you're doing it wrong." | "If you're still charging your phone with the cable that came in the box, you're doing it wrong." |
| Shocking Stat | "[Specific number] of [people] are [doing/suffering X] — and most [authority] never [fix it]." | "72% of Americans are deficient in this one vitamin — and most doctors never test for it." |
| Bold Claim | "This $[price] [thing] replaced [X things] I was paying $[price] for." | "This $29 gadget replaced 4 things I was paying $200 a month for." |

**Rule:** Write 10 hooks for every ad before choosing one. The first 3 will be average. The winner is usually between 7 and 10.

---

## 2. Static vs. Video

**Static wins when:**
- Direct response offer where the image communicates the full value prop
- E-commerce product that is visually compelling on its own
- Retargeting — warm audiences don't need a 30-second education
- You need to test a new offer fast (hours to produce, not days)

**Static formula:** One clear visual + bold headline stating the benefit or calling out the problem + subheadline handling the main objection. No clutter.

**Video wins when:**
- New cold audiences who don't know you yet (builds trust faster)
- Complex products that need demonstration
- Storytelling — before/after, transformation narratives
- UGC-style content that looks native to the feed

**Video rules:**
- Keep under 60 seconds for cold traffic
- Hook must work with no sound — 85% of Facebook videos are watched on mute
- Captions are not optional

**Rule:** Always run at least one static and one video variation in every test. Never assume. Let the data decide.

---

## 3. UGC — The Format That's Printing

UGC (User Generated Content) works because it looks like organic content, not an ad. People's guards are down.

**The UGC timing formula:**

| Timing | What to Do |
|--------|-----------|
| 0:00–0:03 | Hook — call out the problem or make a bold claim |
| 0:03–0:15 | Agitate the problem — why it matters, cost of not solving it |
| 0:15–0:35 | Introduce the solution naturally, like telling a friend |
| 0:35–0:50 | Social proof — what happened when you used it |
| 0:50–0:60 | CTA — direct, simple, one action |

**Delivery:** Slight imperfections (a pause, a laugh, an "um") increase trust. It should feel grabbed-phone, not scripted.

**Where to find creators:** Billo, UGC Ads, X, Facebook niche groups. Budget $50–$150 per video.

**AI UGC rule:** AI is fine for b-roll, product functionality, model wearing product. Never use AI to fake a review — policy violation.

**Rule:** Produce 3 UGC variations with different hooks, same core message. The winner is almost never the one you predicted.

---

## 4. Ad Copy Structure

**The copy structure that converts:**
1. Line 1: Hook — continues the energy of the visual hook
2. Lines 2–3: Problem — in the customer's language, not yours
3. Lines 4–5: Shift — why your solution is different from what they've tried
4. Line 6: Social proof — one specific result from one real person
5. Line 7: CTA — one clear action, no options

**The most important line is Line 1.** Facebook truncates copy after 2–3 lines on mobile. If the first line doesn't earn the click, nobody reads the rest.

**Copy mistakes that kill performance:**
- Writing features instead of outcomes ("advanced AI technology" vs. "saves 3 hours a week")
- Using your language instead of theirs — go read competitor ad comments, Reddit threads, niche Facebook groups. Steal their exact phrases.
- Weak CTAs — "Learn more" is not a CTA. "Claim your spot before they're gone" is a CTA.
- Too long for cold traffic — short and punchy for cold, long form for retargeting

**Rule:** Read your copy out loud before publishing. If it sounds like an ad, rewrite it until it sounds like a conversation.

---

## 5. Creative Testing Protocol

**One variable at a time.** If you change the hook, image, and copy simultaneously, you have no idea what moved the needle.

**Testing structure:**
- New account: ABO (Ad Set Budget Optimization) — CBO starves new creatives before they have enough data
- Minimum spend: $20–$50/day per ad set
- Minimum time: 3–5 days before making decisions. Anything less is reading noise, not signal

**The Plug's exact method:**
1. New account → ABO → minimum 4–5 ad sets
2. Each ad set = 1 different angle
3. Inside each ad set = same video with 3 different scroll stoppers (first 1.7 seconds)
4. Once winner found → transition to CBO
5. All future testing goes inside CBO
6. Test every day: 1 video × 3 different scroll stoppers
7. 1 CBO per country

**What to test — in order:**

| Priority | Element | Why |
|----------|---------|-----|
| 1 | Hook (first 3 sec / headline) | Biggest lever on CTR and cost per click |
| 2 | Creative format (static vs video) | Tells you what your audience responds to |
| 3 | Main image or video thumbnail | Determines scroll-stop |
| 4 | Primary copy (first 2 lines) | Second biggest lever on conversion rate |
| 5 | CTA button text | Small lift, easy to test in parallel |
| 6 | Creative angle | Changes who the ad resonates with |

**Rule:** Launch a new creative test every single day. Your best performer today has an expiration date — sometimes a week, sometimes a few days.

---

## 6. The 6 Creative Angles

Two ads with identical targeting and budget will produce completely different results if one hits the right emotional angle. Rotate angles because different people in the same audience respond to different emotional triggers.

| Angle | Formula | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Fear** | What happens if they don't act? | "Every day you run ads on a flagged account is a day you're one review away from losing everything." |
| **Aspiration** | Paint the picture of success | "What would your business look like if your ads never went down?" |
| **Curiosity** | Open a loop they have to close | "The Facebook account mistake 9 out of 10 advertisers make without knowing it." |
| **Social Proof** | Someone like them already won | "He was banned twice. Third time, he did this instead." |
| **Contrarian** | Challenge a commonly held belief | "Targeting matters less than you think. Here's what actually drives results." |
| **Urgency** | Real scarcity or time pressure | "Q4 ban waves start in October. You have 3 weeks to get this right." |

**Rule:** Run the same offer through at least 3 different angles before deciding the offer doesn't work. Sometimes the offer is fine and the angle is wrong.

---

## 7. Creative Fatigue — Warning Signs & Fixes

**Warning signs:**
- Frequency above 3–4 per week per person
- CTR declining while impressions stay flat
- CPMs rising without targeting changes
- Comment section turning negative (people tagging friends sarcastically)
- Competitors running the same ad concept at scale

**How to extend a winner's life:**
- **New hook, same body** — often resets performance without rebuilding the entire ad
- **New format** — take winning video concept, make it a static (or vice versa)
- **New face** — same script, different UGC creator. Fresh face, same proven message
- **New angle** — reframe the winning offer through a different emotional lens

**Rule:** Never scale an ad without already having its replacement in testing. By the time you notice fatigue, you're already behind.

---

## 8. Benchmarks

| Metric | Bad | Good | Great |
|--------|-----|------|-------|
| CTR (Link) | < 0.5% | 1–2% | > 2% |
| Video Hook Rate (3-sec) | < 20% | 25–35% | > 40% |
| Video Completion Rate | < 15% | 20–35% | > 40% |
| CPM (cold traffic, US) | > $25 | $15–25 | < $15 |
| Thumb-Stop Rate | < 20% | 25–40% | > 40% |
| Frequency (weekly) | > 5x | 2–4x | < 2x |

These are US averages. Calibrate to your own niche data over time.

# Reel Script Testing — Hook Scoring Learnings

Source: Internal script scoring across 14 Pillar 1 reel scripts (June 2026)

---

## The Scoring Framework

Every hook is scored on four factors. Miss the top two and the script will not break 80/100.

| Factor | Weight | What to look for |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| **Specificity** | 40% | A dollar figure AND a timeframe in the first 5 seconds |
| **Audience mirror** | 35% | Viewer sees themselves in sentence one — their pain, their reality |
| **Proof credibility** | 15% | Real names, real revenue, hard-to-fake specifics |
| **Hook type strength** | 10% | Based on tested hook performance rankings |

---

## Hook Type Performance Rankings

| Rank | Hook Type | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| #1 | Credibility Proof | Compressed story arc + name drop in first 10 seconds. Best for cold traffic. |
| #2 | Shocking Stat + Audience Mirror | Stat creates scale. Mirror line makes viewer feel it personally. |
| #3 | Social Proof | Extreme specificity (age, timeframe, $) removes "I'm not qualified" objection before it's raised. |
| #4 | Curiosity / Exposé | Dollar figure + mystery. Must frame from viewer's perspective, not brand's. |
| #5 | Quiet Money | "No product, no following, no office" — removes three objections in one line. |
| #6 | Contradiction | Strong but needs $ + timeframe in hook, not just the body. |
| #7 | Fear | Powerful emotion but only scores high when math hits in present tense, not future. |
| #8 | Aspirational Identity | Better for warm/retargeting. Slow for cold traffic. |
| #9 | Algorithm Validation | Overused. Audiences conditioned to scroll past it. |
| #10 | Freedom / Identity | Works for lifestyle/grid content. Not a conversion hook. |

---

## The Three Most Common Reasons a Hook Scores Under 80

**1. No dollar figure or timeframe in the hook.**
The body has the numbers but the hook doesn't. Cold traffic won't wait — if the promise isn't in the first 5 seconds, they scroll. This is the single biggest lever. Worth 40% of the score.

**2. Framed from the wrong perspective.**
Hooks that lead with the brand's problem ("brands are losing money on ads") instead of the viewer's problem score lower on audience mirror. Always ask: *does the viewer see themselves in sentence one?*

Example of wrong framing: "Right now brands are losing money on every dollar they spend on ads."
Fix: "Right now a brand is paying $3,000 a month to someone managing their ads remotely — and that person could be you."

**3. Future loss instead of present loss.**
"In 10 years you'll be in the same job" is weaker than "you worked a full year and you're making less than 12 months ago." Present tense math hits harder — the viewer feels the loss *right now*.

Weak: "Every year you stay in a job, your earning ceiling gets lower."
Strong: "Average salary raise this year: 3%. Average inflation: 4%. You worked a full year and you're making less than you were 12 months ago."

---

## The Audience Mirror Line

Every high-scoring hook contains a line that names something the viewer feels but hasn't said out loud:

- "Most people feel this but can't name it." (Fear hook)
- "Most people never question it." (Shocking stat hook)
- "They're ready for something different." (Quiet money hook)
- "That's not unusual. That's the pattern I keep seeing." (Social proof hook)

This line does two things: validates the viewer's feeling and positions you as someone who understands them before you've asked for anything. Without it, even a strong hook reads like a pitch instead of a conversation.
